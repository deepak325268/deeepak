Question 1: What is second language learning? Explain. (2023.S)

Introduction:

In India, we learn many languages. The language we learn at home from our parents is called the mother tongue or first language (like Hindi, Maithili, or Bhojpuri). When we learn a new language after our mother tongue, it is called "Second Language Learning". For Indian students, learning English in school is the best example of second language learning. It is a very important process for our education.

Main Points:

  • Needs Hard Work (Conscious Effort): We learn our mother tongue naturally without reading any books. We just listen to our parents and start speaking. But to learn a second language, we have to try very hard. We have to pay attention in the classroom and study.
  • Formal Environment: We can learn our mother tongue anywhere, like in the playground or at home. But we learn a second language mostly in a formal place like a school or a coaching class. We need a proper teacher, a blackboard, and textbooks to learn it.
  • Learning Rules and Grammar: When we learn a second language, we cannot just speak anything. We have to learn its strict rules. We have to learn correct spelling, grammar (like tense, noun, verb), and vocabulary (word meanings).
  • Target Language: The new language that we want to learn is called the 'Target Language'. In our schools, English is our target language. We often use our mother tongue to understand the rules of the target language.
  • Purpose of Learning: People learn a second language for many important reasons. We learn English to get good jobs, go to big colleges for higher education, use mobile phones and the internet, and talk to people from other states and countries.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, second language learning is a very important educational process. It does not happen automatically like the mother tongue. A student has to work hard, practice daily, and take the help of a good teacher to learn the second language properly.

Question 2: What are the principles of second language learning? Or What do you understand by the principle of active participation in language learning? (2021.S, 2022.S)

Introduction:

Teaching a language is an art. To teach English properly, teachers use some basic rules or ideas. These rules are called the "Principles of Language Learning." These principles help the teacher to make the class interesting. They also help the students to learn the language very easily and quickly without getting bored.

Main Principles:

  • Principle of Active Participation: This is a very important principle. It means students cannot learn English just by sitting quietly and listening to the teacher. They must actively participate in the class. They should speak out loud, ask questions, play games, do role-plays, and sing rhymes. The more they participate, the more they learn.
  • Principle of Habit Formation: Learning a language is like making a new habit. If we want to learn how to ride a bicycle, we have to practice daily. In the same way, students must practice speaking and reading English daily. They should practice it so much that speaking English becomes a natural habit.
  • Principle of Motivation: Motivation means creating interest. A child will only learn if he is interested in the subject. The teacher must use stories, games, and colorful pictures to motivate the students. If a student is motivated, he will learn English very fast.
  • Principle of Natural Order (LSRW): A small baby first Listens, then Speaks, then Reads, and then Writes. This is the natural order of learning. English should also be taught in this exact order: Listening (L), Speaking (S), Reading (R), and Writing (W). We should not force children to write before they know how to listen and speak.
  • Principle of Exposure: Students should get an English environment. The teacher should put English charts on the walls and use simple English words in the classroom (like "Come here", "Sit down") so that students hear English everywhere.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, the principles of second language learning act as a guide for the teacher. If a teacher follows these principles, specially the principle of active participation, the students will learn English very easily without any fear.

Question 3: Discuss the principles of teaching second language with suitable examples. (2022.L)

Introduction:

The principles of teaching guide a teacher on how to teach a lesson in the classroom. English is a foreign language. When teaching English to primary children, the teacher must use simple methods and give daily life examples so that children can easily understand the new language.

Main Principles with Examples:

  • Principle of Linking with Life: The teacher should connect English words with the child's real daily life.
    Example: Instead of teaching hard dictionary words, the teacher should ask students, "What did you eat for breakfast today?" and teach them words like "Milk," "Apple," or "Bread."
  • Principle of Using Real Objects: Children understand better when they see things with their own eyes. The teacher should bring real items to the class.
    Example: To teach the word 'Round' and 'Red', the teacher should bring a real red ball or an apple to the class and show it to the students.
  • Principle of Oral Approach: Speaking should come before writing. Children learn easily by speaking and singing together.
    Example: Before telling the children to write A, B, C, D in their copies, the teacher should make them sing the A, B, C, D song loudly in the class.
  • Principle of Simple to Complex: The teacher should start with very easy things and then slowly teach hard things.
    Example: The teacher should first teach simple 3-letter words like "Bat" and "Cat." After the students learn this, the teacher should teach long words like "Elephant" or "Umbrella."
  • Principle of Multiple Methods: All children in a class are not the same. So, the teacher should use different styles to teach.
    Example: One day the teacher can tell a story, the next day they can play a vocabulary game on the blackboard, and the third day they can sing a poem.

Conclusion:

To conclude, teaching principles are very useful for an English teacher. By using real-life examples and starting from simple things, the teacher makes the English language very friendly, easy, and interesting for primary students.

Question 4: What do you understand by the principle of 'learning with habitat' in language curriculum? (2022.S)

Introduction:

The word "Habitat" means the local environment, village, or society where a child lives. "Learning with habitat" is a very important principle in the language curriculum. It means that the English syllabus and teaching should be connected to the child's local area, family, and daily experiences.

Main Points:

  • Local Culture in Textbooks: If the English book only talks about foreign countries, snow, or foreign foods, the village child will not understand anything. The book should have stories about local festivals like Chhath Puja, Diwali, Eid, or local village fairs. This makes learning easy.
  • Easy and Known Vocabulary: When learning a new language, the words should be about things the child sees every day. The teacher should teach the English names of local trees (like Mango tree, Banyan tree), local animals (like Cow, Goat), and things found in their own houses.
  • Removes the Fear of English: Village children often feel scared of English. They think it is a very hard language. But when they see that the English book is talking about their own village and their own festivals, their fear goes away. They feel that English is a friendly language.
  • Connecting Home and School: When the teaching is based on habitat, the child can practice English at home too. They can point to a tree in their village and tell their parents, "This is a tree." It builds a bridge between the school learning and the home environment.
  • Respect for Local Life: This principle teaches children that their local culture is very important. They learn how to describe their own village, food, and culture in an international language like English.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, learning with habitat makes English very relatable and easy for the students. It proves that English is not just an alien language, but it is a useful tool that children can use to talk about their own beautiful world.

Question 5: What are the objectives of teaching English as a second language?

Introduction:

"Objective" means the main goal or aim. English is a global language. It is very important for communication, higher studies, and getting good jobs. But in primary school, we do not teach English to make students big scholars. We teach English with some basic and practical objectives so they can use it in their daily life.

Main Objectives:

  • To Develop Listening Skills: The first objective is to make sure students can hear and understand English. When the teacher says "Stand up," "Sit down," or "Open your book," the student should understand it. They should also understand simple English stories and poems when they listen to them.
  • To Develop Speaking Skills: Many Indian students can write English very well but cannot speak. The objective is to remove their hesitation. Students should be able to speak simple sentences like "May I come in, Sir?" or "My name is Rahul," without any fear or shame.
  • To Develop Reading Skills: Students should learn how to recognize English letters and words. The objective is to make them read simple storybooks, English signboards on the road, and their textbook chapters with correct pronunciation and speed.
  • To Develop Writing Skills: Students must learn the mechanics of writing. The objective is to teach them how to hold a pencil, write alphabets in four-line copies, use capital letters and full stops, and write simple paragraphs or school leave applications.
  • To Build Basic Vocabulary: The objective is to teach students the English words for daily use. They should know the English names of body parts, colors, domestic animals, relations (mother, father), and classroom items (pen, blackboard) so they can use them in their daily communication.

Conclusion:

To sum up, the main objective of teaching English at the primary level is to build a strong foundation. The goal is to develop the four main skills—Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing (LSRW)—so that the child feels confident and ready for the future.

Question 7: Discuss the factors affecting second language learning? Or Explain the development, socio-economic and psychological factors affecting second language learning. (2020.S, 2023.L)

Introduction:

Learning English as a second language is not equally easy for all students. In a classroom, some students learn English very fast, while some students struggle a lot. This happens because there are many different reasons or "factors" that affect how a child learns a new language.

Main Factors Affecting Second Language Learning:

  • Psychological Factors (Mind and Feelings):
    - Motivation: If a child is interested and wants to learn English, he will learn very fast.
    - Fear and Hesitation: Many village students feel scared that others will laugh at them if they speak wrong English. This fear stops them from learning.
  • Socio-Economic Factors (Home and Money Background):
    - Family Income: Rich families can send their children to good English medium schools or buy good books and smartphones. Poor families cannot afford these, which makes learning English hard for their children.
    - Educated Parents: If parents know English, they can help the child with homework and talk to them in English at home.
  • Environmental Factors (Exposure): A child learns fast if he hears English everywhere. In big cities, children see English signboards and watch English cartoons. But in a village, there is no English environment outside the school. This slows down their learning.
  • Teaching Factors: If the teacher is friendly and uses games, pictures, and stories, the students learn very fast. If the teacher is very strict and only uses the translation method, the students get bored.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, learning a second language depends on many things like the child's mind, their family background, and the school environment. A good teacher should understand all these factors and help the weak students with extra care.

Question 8: Discuss the factors in your opinion that may affect learning of English or second language? Or What are the developmental factors affecting second language learning? (2020.S, 2024.S)

Introduction:

As a child grows up, his body, mind, and habits change. This growth is called "development." When a child is learning English as a second language, his age and mental growth (developmental factors) play a very big role in how fast or slow he learns.

Main Developmental Factors:

  • The Age Factor (Right Time to Learn): Science says that childhood is the best time to learn a new language. Small children have a fresh brain like a sponge. They can easily copy the correct pronunciation of English words. Older children learn grammar rules better, but they struggle with pronunciation.
  • Cognitive Development (Brain Power): "Cognitive" means related to the brain. A small child can easily learn simple words like "Apple" or "Cat" by looking at pictures. But to understand hard grammar rules like "Tense" or "Active-Passive voice," the child's brain needs to grow and mature.
  • Physical Development (Motor Skills): To learn the writing skill in English, the child needs physical development. The muscles of their fingers must be strong enough to hold a pencil properly and write ABCDs neatly in the copy.
  • Emotional Development: Small children are very bold. They speak English words without any shame, even if they are wrong. But as they grow into teenagers, they become emotionally sensitive. They feel very shy and scared to speak English in front of their friends.
  • Mother Tongue Habit: As the child grows older, the habit of speaking the mother tongue (like Hindi or Bhojpuri) becomes very strong. This strong habit sometimes creates problems, as the child tries to use Hindi rules while speaking English.

Conclusion:

To conclude, a teacher must always keep the child's age and mental development in mind. We cannot teach hard grammar to a small child. Teaching should always match the developmental level of the students.

Question 9: What do you mean by curriculum? (2021.S)

Introduction:

In simple words, a curriculum is the complete plan of a school. Many people think that 'Syllabus' and 'Curriculum' are the same thing, but they are very different. The syllabus only tells us about the books and chapters, but the curriculum is a much bigger concept. It includes everything that happens in the school.

Main Points of Curriculum:

  • Total School Environment: The curriculum includes every activity a student does inside and outside the classroom. It includes reading books, playing sports, morning assembly, drawing, and cultural programs like Independence Day.
  • All-Round Development: The main aim of the curriculum is the all-round development of the child. It does not only want to make the child a bookworm (किताबी कीड़ा). It focuses on making the child physically strong, mentally sharp, and morally good.
  • Syllabus is a Small Part: The syllabus is just a small part of the curriculum. The syllabus tells us what to teach (like Math or English chapters). But the curriculum tells the teacher how to teach and why to teach it.
  • Dynamic in Nature: Curriculum is never fixed forever. It changes with time. For example, twenty years ago, there were no computers in schools. But now, computer education is a part of the curriculum because society needs it.
  • Hidden Curriculum: There are many things not written in textbooks, like discipline, respecting teachers, sharing tiffin with friends, and teamwork. Students learn these good habits from the school environment. This is called the hidden curriculum.

Conclusion:

In short, the curriculum is a complete map or blueprint for the school. It guides the teachers and the principal to provide the best environment so that students can become smart and good citizens of the country.

Question 10: What are the objectives of teaching of English at the elementary level with reference to National Curriculum Framework 2005?

Introduction:

NCF 2005 stands for the National Curriculum Framework 2005. It brought very big and good changes to the Indian education system. It said that learning should be joyful and without the burden of heavy school bags. NCF 2005 gave some very clear objectives (goals) for teaching English to primary students.

Main Objectives according to NCF 2005:

  • Basic Communication: The main goal of teaching English is not to make students grammar experts. The goal is that students should be able to understand simple English, follow the teacher's instructions, and talk about their daily needs in simple English.
  • Using the Mother Tongue (Multilingualism): NCF 2005 strictly says that teachers should not ban the mother tongue in the English class. The teacher should use the child's home language (like Hindi or Bhojpuri) to explain difficult English words easily.
  • Connecting English to Real Life: English lessons should not be about foreign countries or snow. The English textbook should have stories about the child's village, local festivals, and local animals so that the child finds English friendly and relatable.
  • Learning Without Fear: Many Indian students are very scared of English. NCF 2005 says the objective is to remove this "English Phobia." The classroom should be a safe place where students can make mistakes without the fear of punishment.
  • Activity-Based Learning: English should not be taught just by reading the textbook. The objective is to teach English through fun activities, games, singing poems, and role-plays. This integrates Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing (LSRW) naturally.

Conclusion:

To conclude, NCF 2005 wants to make English a fun and useful language for Indian students. The main objective is to give children the confidence to use English happily, without taking any stress or burden.

Conclusion:

Question 13: Describe guiding principles of NCF 2005 and BCF 2008 in the teaching of English.

Introduction:

Both NCF 2005 (National level) and BCF 2008 (Bihar level) have the same main thinking. They both want to make education stress-free and child-centered. They have given some very important guiding principles for teachers to follow in the classroom.

  • Connect Knowledge to Outside Life: Do not just teach from the book. Connect the English lessons to the real world, like the child's home, the playground, and the village.

  • Nurturing Democratic Values: The classroom should treat all students equally. Boys, girls, rich, poor, and children speaking different mother tongues should sit together and respect each other.

Conclusion:

In short, the guiding principles of NCF 2005 and BCF 2008 focus on the happiness of the child. They guide the teacher to make English learning practical, real, and completely free from the burden of exams and rote memory.

Question 14: Describe the curriculum, syllabus, textbook of English in Bihar at primary level. Also differentiate among them. Or Mention the relationship between curriculum, syllabus and textbook.

Introduction:

Many times, people get confused between Curriculum, Syllabus, and Textbook. They think all three are the same. But in the education system, they are three very different things. To understand how a school works, we must understand the difference between them.

1. Curriculum (The Big Plan):

  • Curriculum is the complete, overall plan of the school.
  • It includes everything: subjects, sports, morning assembly, cultural programs, and moral values.
  • Its main goal is the all-round development (physical, mental, and social) of the child. It is like the 'Blueprint' of a building.

2. Syllabus (The Subject List):

  • Syllabus is a small part of the curriculum.
  • It is just a list of topics or chapters that a teacher has to teach in one specific subject (like English) in one academic year.
  • It only focuses on bookish knowledge and exams.

3. Textbook (The Tool):

  • A textbook is the printed book given to the students (For example: 'Blossom' for English in Bihar).
  • It contains stories, poems, and exercises based exactly on the syllabus.
  • It is a physical tool in the hands of the student and the teacher to complete the syllabus.

Relationship Between Them:

Think of three circles. The biggest outer circle is the Curriculum. Inside the curriculum is a smaller circle called the Syllabus. And inside the syllabus is the smallest circle called the Textbook. The textbook follows the syllabus, and the syllabus follows the curriculum.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, curriculum is the complete journey of education, syllabus is the map of topics, and the textbook is the vehicle we use to complete that journey. All three must work together for good teaching.

Question 16: What do you understand by the principle of 'simple to complex' in a language curriculum? (2023.S)

Introduction:

When teaching any new subject, a teacher must follow a path. The principle of 'simple to complex' is a very important rule in teaching English. It simply means that the teacher should teach easy things first and then slowly move to hard things.

Main Points:

  • Easy Vocabulary First: A teacher should not teach long words like "Environment" or "Beautiful" on the first day. First, teach simple 3-letter words that children see daily, like "Cat," "Dog," "Bat," and "Pen."
  • Simple Grammar First: In grammar, teach simple things first. For example, teach simple present tense like "I play" before teaching complex grammar like "I have been playing."
  • Small Sentences First: Do not tell children to write long paragraphs. First, teach them to make small sentences like "This is a book." Once they learn this, they can make long sentences.
  • Boosts Confidence: English is a new language, so children are scared of it. When the teacher starts with easy things, children give the right answers. This makes them very happy and confident.
  • Like Climbing Stairs: Learning English is like climbing stairs. You cannot jump directly to the 10th step. You have to climb the 1st step, then the 2nd step. This step-by-step learning is called 'simple to complex'.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, the principle of 'simple to complex' makes learning English completely stress-free. It builds a very strong foundation for the child so that they can easily understand hard topics in the future.

Question 17: What are the different approaches to teaching of English? Which approach is appropriate to teach a story? (2020.S, 2021.L)

Introduction:

An "approach" means a style or a method of teaching. Every teacher has a different style of teaching English. Over many years, experts have made different approaches to teach English in the best way.

Different Approaches to Teaching English:

  • Grammar-Translation Method: In this method, the teacher reads an English line and translates it into Hindi or the mother tongue. It focuses heavily on grammar rules and book reading.
  • Audio-Lingual Approach: In this method, children listen to an audio or the teacher and repeat it again and again loudly. It is like forming a habit through repeating.
  • Structural Approach: Here, the focus is on sentence structure. Children practice one pattern many times, like "This is a pen," "This is a book," "This is a cat."
  • Communicative Approach: In this modern approach, the main focus is on talking. The teacher creates situations (like buying vegetables) and students have to talk in English to complete the task.

Which approach is appropriate to teach a story?

To teach a story to small children, the Communicative and Constructivist Approaches are the best.
A story is for fun, imagination, and sharing ideas. The teacher should not use the Grammar-Translation method because checking grammar will kill the fun of the story. The teacher should use the Communicative approach to ask simple questions like, "What do you think the lion will do now?" This makes the children speak and enjoy the story.

Conclusion:

To conclude, there are many styles of teaching, but a teacher must choose the style based on the topic. For stories and poems, approaches that encourage speaking and imagination are always the best.

Introduction:

There is no single "perfect" method to teach English. Every teaching method has its own special benefits (advantages). A good teacher uses a mix of all these methods depending on what the children need.

Benefits of Different Approaches:

  • Communicative Approach: Its biggest benefit is that it removes the fear of speaking. It makes children confident in talking to others in real life.
  • Structural Approach: It helps children learn how to make correct English sentences without making mistakes in the word order.
  • Constructivist Approach: It makes children think. They do not just copy the teacher; they do activities and discover the meaning themselves. This keeps the knowledge in their brain for a long time.
  • Audio-Lingual Method: Its biggest benefit is correct pronunciation. By listening and repeating, children learn the correct sound of English words.
  • Grammar Translation Method: It is very helpful for learning vocabulary. Children easily understand the exact meaning of hard English words in their mother tongue (Hindi/Bhojpuri).
  • Cognitive Approach: It helps older students understand the logic and rules behind the language, making them smart thinkers.
  • Behaviouristic Approach: It is good for small children. Using rewards (like a star or clapping) makes them form good habits of learning.

Conclusion:

In short, every approach is like a different tool in a teacher's toolbox. While modern times focus on communication, older methods like translation and drilling still help in building vocabulary and pronunciation.

It is a step-by-step guide. It has mainly four parts: 1. Introduction (Waking up the students' interest). 2. Presentation (Teaching the new topic). 3. Practice (Making students do activities). 4. Assessment (Checking if they understood).

Question 36: What do you mean by skimming and scanning? How can it be used to develop reading skills?

Introduction:

When we read a book, we do not always read every single word slowly. Sometimes we are in a hurry and we just want to read fast. To read very fast, we use two special techniques (tricks). These two techniques are called "Skimming" and "Scanning". They are very important sub-skills of reading.

1. Skimming (Getting the Main Idea):

  • Meaning: Skimming means flying your eyes quickly over the page just to understand the main topic or the "gist" (summary) of the chapter. You do not read every word. You skip the hard words and long details.
  • Example: In the morning, our grandfather reads the newspaper. He does not read every single line of the whole newspaper. He just looks at the big headlines and pictures to know what happened in the country. This is called skimming.

2. Scanning (Finding Specific Information):

  • Meaning: Scanning means looking through a text very fast only to find one specific detail (like a name, a date, or a number). You completely ignore all the other lines and words.
  • Example: Imagine your 10th board exam result is published in a newspaper on a page with 10,000 names. You do not read every name. Your eyes quickly move to search for only your name or your roll number. This fast searching is called scanning.

How They Develop Reading Skills:

  • Increases Reading Speed: Many students have a bad habit of reading word-by-word slowly. Skimming and scanning break this bad habit. It trains their eyes to move very fast on the paper.
  • Saves Time: In examinations, there is an unseen passage with questions. If the student reads the whole passage slowly, time will be over. Scanning helps the student find the exact answer quickly.
  • Improves Brain Focus: Scanning trains the brain to ignore useless information and focus only on what is important.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, skimming and scanning are not just school tricks; they are daily life survival skills. A good teacher must practice these fast-reading techniques in class so that students can become smart and fast readers.

Question 37: How skimming and scanning can be done?

Introduction:

Knowing the definition of skimming and scanning is not enough. A teacher must also know the physical tricks and eye movements required to do it. Students do not learn it automatically; the teacher has to give them step-by-step training on how to move their eyes fast.

How Skimming Can Be Done (Steps):

  • Read the Title: The title or the heading is the most important thing. It immediately tells you what the chapter is about.
  • Look at Pictures and Bold Words: Pictures, charts, and words written in bold or italics give a very quick summary of the page.
  • Read the First Paragraph: The first paragraph is the introduction. It tells you the main topic. Read it fully.
  • Read the First Line of Middle Paragraphs: In the middle paragraphs, do not read everything. Just read the first sentence of every paragraph and skip the rest.
  • Read the Last Paragraph: The last paragraph is the conclusion. It gives the final message of the writer.

How Scanning Can Be Done (Steps):

  • Fix the Target: Before looking at the book, the student must be 100% clear about what he wants to find. Does he want a Date? A Name? A Place? A Price?
  • Look for Clues (Capital Letters and Numbers): If the target is a name or a place, the eyes should only search for Capital letters (like Delhi, Rahul). If the target is a year, the eyes should search for numbers (like 1947).
  • Use the Finger Guide: Beginners should use their index finger or a pencil. Move the pencil very fast on the paper in a zigzag style. The eyes will follow the pencil.
  • Stop and Read: When the eyes catch the target word, stop moving. Then read the full sentence around that word to check if it is the correct answer.

Conclusion:

To sum up, doing skimming and scanning is like an exercise for the eyes and the brain. With regular practice in the classroom, the students' eyes become very sharp and they can catch important information in a few seconds.

Question 44: What do you mean by mechanics of writing? (2020.S)

Introduction:

When we think about writing a story, we think about imagination and ideas. But before a child can write a beautiful story, his hands and fingers must know how to put words clearly on the paper. The basic, technical, and physical rules of writing on a paper are called the "Mechanics of Writing."

Main Components (Parts) of Mechanics of Writing:

  • Handwriting and Pencil Grip (Motor Skills): This is the physical part. The child must know how to hold the pencil properly with three fingers (tripod grip) so that his hand does not get tired. He must know how to make round circles and straight lines to form beautiful ABCDs on a four-line copy.
  • Correct Spelling: A very important part of mechanics is spelling. English has very tricky spellings. If a student writes "Write" as "Right", the whole meaning changes. Memorizing the correct spelling is necessary for good writing.
  • Use of Punctuation Marks: Punctuation marks are like traffic signals for a sentence. They tell the reader where to stop and where to wait. Using Full Stop (.), Comma (,), and Question Mark (?) correctly is a major part of writing mechanics. Without them, writing is useless.
  • Rules of Capital Letters: The student must know the strict rules of English capitalization. For example, the first letter of a new sentence must be a Big Letter. Special names of people and cities (like Ram, Delhi) must always start with a Capital letter.
  • Proper Spacing and Layout: Good writing must look beautiful to the eyes. The mechanics include the "One-finger space rule". The child must leave a small gap between two words so they do not mix together. He must also know how to leave margins on the paper and write in straight lines.

Conclusion:

Ultimately, the mechanics of writing act like the foundation of a house. Just as you cannot build a strong house without bricks and cement, a student cannot become a good English writer without mastering good handwriting, correct spelling, and proper punctuation.

Question 51: Explain writing as a Process. Also explain the stages of writing process that is brainstorming, mind map, drafting, evaluation reviewing, editing and final draft.

Introduction:

In the old days, teachers used to say, "Write an essay on a Cow," and the students had to write it perfectly in one go. This is called the "Product approach." But today, we know that writing is not a one-time magic trick. Writing is a slow journey. A writer thinks, writes, makes mistakes, and writes again. This step-by-step journey is called "Writing as a Process."

Stages of the Writing Process:

  • 1. Brainstorming (Pre-writing): This is the first step. Before touching the pen, the child just thinks. The brain generates many random ideas, words, and thoughts about the topic. There is no rule here; just think of anything related to the topic.
  • 2. Mind Map (Organizing Ideas): Now, the child takes a rough paper and draws a circle in the middle with the main topic (like "My Dog"). Then, he draws lines (branches) to organize the random words. It looks like a map of ideas.
  • 3. Drafting (Rough Writing): This is the first attempt to write sentences. The golden rule here is: Do not worry about spelling or grammar. The child just writes down his ideas on paper as fast as possible. This is called a rough draft.
  • 4. Evaluation / Reviewing (Checking the Content): The child (or his friend) reads the rough draft. They check if the story is good. They ask, "Did I forget any important point?" or "Is the story making sense?" They focus only on the meaning.
  • 5. Editing (Fixing the Mistakes): Now, the child becomes a strict checker. He takes a red pen and fixes the "Mechanics of writing." He corrects the wrong spellings, adds capital letters, and fixes punctuation marks (like full stops).
  • 6. Final Draft (Publishing): This is the last step. The child writes the fully corrected essay in his best and neatest handwriting on a fresh page. It is now ready to be checked by the teacher.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, treating writing as a process breaks a very big, scary task into small, easy steps. It teaches the students that making mistakes in the rough draft is a good thing, and good writing comes only after careful planning and checking.

  • Meaning: Functional grammar means learning grammar for "Daily Function" or real-life use. It focuses on how language is used to share messages and feelings in society. NCF 2005 strongly supports this.

  • Result: Students become very confident. They learn how to talk to a shopkeeper, how to talk to a teacher, and how to use English in the real world.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, Traditional grammar is like a dry skeleton of bones, while Functional grammar is like a living human body. Today, we do not need students who only know definitions; we need students who can communicate functionally.

Question 66: Differentiate between inductive and deductive modes of teaching Grammar. (2023.S)

Differences between Inductive and Deductive Modes:

  • 1. The Basic Direction (Path of Learning):
    • Inductive Mode: This method moves from Examples to Rules. It is a bottom-up approach.
    • Deductive Mode: This method moves from Rules to Examples. It is a top-down approach.
  • 2. How the Teacher Teaches (The Process):
    • Inductive Mode: The teacher does not write any formula on the board. The teacher gives 10 simple examples (like "Cats, Dogs, Pens"). The students use their brains, look at the examples, and act like detectives to find the hidden rule (adding 's' makes plural).

  • 3. The Role of the Learner (Active vs Passive):
    • Inductive Mode: The students are highly active. They are thinkers and problem solvers. They discover the knowledge themselves.
    • Deductive Mode: The students are mostly passive. They just sit quietly, listen to the teacher, and act like a machine that memorizes information.
  • 4. Memory and Retention:
    • Inductive Mode: Because the students discover the rule using their own brain, they never forget it. The memory lasts for a whole lifetime.
    • Deductive Mode: Because the students only memorize the rule blindly, they forget it very quickly after the exam is over.
  • 5. Suitability (For which class?):
    • Inductive Mode: This is the best and most perfect method for small children (Primary level) because they learn easily through real examples and stories.
    • Deductive Mode: This method is suitable only for older students (High School or College) who have a mature brain to understand hard formulas.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, the deductive mode is very fast and easy for the teacher, but it is very boring for the students. Modern education (like NCF 2005) strongly supports the inductive mode because it makes grammar learning a joyful discovery, not a heavy burden.

To sum up, the approach to teaching grammar has shifted from rigid rote-memorization to flexible, communication-based learning. An expert teacher uses the Eclectic approach to ensure that students learn the rules and also learn how to speak confidently.

Question 70: Define distinctive sounds of English i.e. intonation and rhythm with examples. (2024.L)

Introduction:

When we listen to an English news anchor or a native speaker, their English sounds very musical. It does not sound flat like a robot. This music is created by special "prosodic features" or distinctive sounds, mainly Intonation and Rhythm. For an Indian student, learning these sounds is just as important as learning grammar.

1. Intonation (The Rise and Fall of Voice):

  • Definition: Intonation means the changing pitch of the voice. When we speak English, our voice goes up (high pitch) and goes down (low pitch) automatically to show our feelings, surprise, or anger, without changing the actual words.
  • Examples:
    • Falling Intonation (For Statements): When we are sure about something or giving an order, our voice goes down at the end of the sentence.
      Example: "I am going to school." (The voice drops on the word 'school').
    • Rising Intonation (For Questions): When we ask a Yes/No question, or when we are surprised, our voice goes up at the end of the sentence.
      Example: "Are you coming?" (The voice rises on the word 'coming').

2. Rhythm (The Beat of the Language):

  • Definition: Rhythm is the musical beat of a language. English is a "stress-timed" language. This means we do not give equal time to every single word. We push (stress) the big, important meaning words very loudly and clearly, and we squeeze or eat up the small grammar words very fast.
  • Examples:
    • Look at this sentence: "The BOY is EATING an APPLE."
    • Here, "BOY", "EATING", and "APPLE" are the important words. We say them very loudly and slowly (Stress). But the words "The", "is", and "an" are small grammar words. We speak them very fast and softly. This mix of loud and soft words creates a beautiful bouncing beat, which is called Rhythm.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, if a student speaks English with perfect grammar but completely flat voice, nobody will understand his feelings. Teachers must actively practice intonation and rhythm through singing rhymes and reciting dialogues so that Indian students can sound highly natural and expressive.

Question 71: What do you mean by word formation? (2024.L)

Introduction:

Language is like a living tree; it always grows and makes new leaves. In the English language, there are lakhs (hundreds of thousands) of words. A student cannot memorize the whole dictionary. "Word formation" is a magic trick of grammar. It is the process of creating completely new words from old, existing base words. If a student learns the rules of word formation, he can easily guess the meaning of new words without opening a dictionary.

Main Processes of Word Formation:

  • 1. Affixation (Adding small parts): This is the most common method. We add a small meaningful part to the main word. It has two types:
    • Prefix (Added at the beginning): It usually changes the meaning to the opposite. Example: Un- + Happy = Unhappy. Dis- + Agree = Disagree.
    • Suffix (Added at the end): It usually changes the 'part of speech' (like changing a verb to a noun). Example: Teach + -er = Teacher. Care + -ful = Careful.
  • 2. Compounding (Joining two words): In this process, we take two completely independent English words and stick them together (like Fevicol) to make a brand-new word with a new meaning. Examples: Sun + Flower = Sunflower. Black + Board = Blackboard. Note + Book = Notebook.
  • 3. Conversion (Zero Derivation - Changing the Job): This is a very interesting trick. Here, we do not add any spelling. We just change the 'job' of the word. A noun is used as a verb. Example: The word "Water" is a noun (Give me a glass of water). But it can be converted into a verb (I water the plants daily).
  • 4. Clipping (Cutting the word): In our fast daily life, we don't like speaking very long words. Clipping means cutting a long word to make it small and easy to speak, without changing its meaning. Examples: Mathematics becomes Math. Gymnasium becomes Gym. Telephone becomes Phone.
  • 5. Blending (Mixing two words): This means taking half parts of two different words and mixing them together in a mixer to create a new concept. Examples: Breakfast + Lunch = Brunch. Smoke + Fog = Smog.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, word formation is the engine of vocabulary growth. By teaching elementary students the simple rules of prefixes, suffixes, and compounding, a teacher gives them the power to multiply their vocabulary very fast without rote learning (रट्टा मारना).

Question 72: Explain different parts of speech in English with suitable examples. (2024.L)

Introduction:

When we build a house, we need different workers like a plumber, an electrician, and a painter. Everyone has a different job. In the exact same way, when we build an English sentence, we need different types of words. According to the "job" or "function" of the words in a sentence, they are divided into 8 groups. These 8 groups are called the "Parts of Speech."

The Eight Parts of Speech with Examples:

  • 1. Noun (The Naming Word): A noun is the name of anything in the world. It can be the name of a person, place, animal, thing, or a feeling. Examples: Ram is a good boy. Patna is a big city. The book is on the table. Honesty is the best policy.
  • 2. Pronoun (The Replacing Word): If we say "Ram is a boy. Ram plays cricket. Ram goes to school," it sounds very boring. To stop this repetition, we use a pronoun in place of a noun. Examples: Ram is a boy. He plays cricket. Other examples: She, It, They, We, I.
  • 3. Adjective (The Describing Word): An adjective acts like makeup. It adds beauty and extra information to a noun or pronoun. It tells us 'how is the noun' (color, size, quality). Examples: A beautiful flower, a tall tree, a red car, three pens.
  • 4. Verb (The Action Word): This is the king of the sentence. No sentence can be made without a verb. It tells us what action is happening or what the state of a person is. Examples (Action): The boy runs. The girl eats an apple. Examples (State): He is happy. They are students.
  • 5. Adverb (The Modifying Word): An adverb tells us how, when, or where an action (verb) happens. It mostly ends with the letters 'ly'. Examples: The old man walks slowly (How does he walk? Slowly). Come here now (When? Now). The tea is very hot.
  • 6. Preposition (The Position Word): A preposition shows the location or relationship of a noun with other words in the sentence. It tells us where a thing is placed. Examples: The pen is in the bag. The cat is hiding under the bed. The clock is on the wall.
  • 7. Conjunction (The Joining Word): A conjunction acts like a bridge or Fevicol. It joins two words, two phrases, or two sentences together. Examples: Ram and Shyam are friends. I want to play, but it is raining. I passed because I worked hard.
  • 8. Interjection (The Feeling Word): An interjection is a short word used to express sudden and strong feelings from the heart, like great happiness, sadness, or surprise. It always takes an exclamation mark (!). Examples: Wow! What a beautiful picture. Alas! We lost the cricket match. Ouch! It hurts.

Conclusion:

To sum up, the eight parts of speech are the basic building blocks of the English language. If a student clearly understands the exact job of these eight workers, he will never make a grammar mistake in his life and will always make perfect sentences.
